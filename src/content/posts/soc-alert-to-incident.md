# From Alert to Incident

An alert is not an incident. It is a question generated by a detection rule, and the analyst's job is to answer that question with evidence.

## Establish the Baseline

Begin with the affected identity, host, time, and detection source. Determine what is normal for that user and system. A PowerShell process on an administrator workstation may be routine; the same process launched from a document reader on a finance endpoint deserves immediate attention.

## Build the Timeline

Look before and after the triggering event. Authentication logs, process ancestry, DNS requests, network connections, file creation, and endpoint telemetry often reveal a sequence that a single alert cannot show.

Ask four questions:

1. What initiated the activity?
2. What changed on the system?
3. Where did the activity communicate?
4. Did it spread or affect another identity?

## Make a Defensible Decision

Document why the activity is benign, suspicious, or malicious. Include the evidence that supports the decision and any remaining uncertainty. High-quality triage is not about closing alerts quickly; it is about reducing uncertainty without losing the facts that matter.

**A strong analyst does not chase noise. A strong analyst reconstructs behavior.**
